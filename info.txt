Here's every scenario in plain terms:                    
                                                                                
  ---                                                                           
  1. no_disruption
  Nothing happens. Normal operations for the full 100 days. This is the baseline
   — used to check that everything works and measure the "peace time" cost of
  each strategy.                                                                
  
  ---                                                                           
  2. short_conflict
  On day 30, Hormuz gets blocked to 50% capacity. It stays that way for 30 days,
   then recovers. Think of it as a brief regional flare-up — ships can still
  pass but it takes twice as long, queues build up.                             
  
  ---                                                                           
  3. medium_blockade
  On day 30, Hormuz drops to 20% capacity for 90 days. More serious — ships take
   5× longer to transit. This is where cost_focused starts failing because it
  doesn't react early enough.                                                   
                  
  ---                                                                           
  4. long_war     
  A full regional war that escalates in stages:
  - Day 15: Hormuz nearly closed (5% — ships take 20× longer)
  - Day 25: Red Sea also nearly closed (10%)                 
  - Day 30: 15 tankers are permanently destroyed (fleet drops from 120 to 105)  
                                                                              
  Both major chokepoints are unusable. Only Africa route survives.              
                                                                                
  ---                                                                           
  5. all_out_war                                                                
  The worst-case scenario. Everything hits at once:
  - Day 0: Russia sanctioned — that supply source is completely cut off
  - Day 7: Hormuz almost completely closed (2%)                                 
  - Day 10: Red Sea almost completely closed (2%)                               
  - Day 14: 65 tankers destroyed — fleet drops from 120 to just 55              
                                                                                
  Only Africa route remains, but with 55 tankers it can't cover full demand.
  Every strategy fails equally here.                                            
                  
  ---                                                                           
  6. cape_disruption
  The scenario designed to stress the Africa "safe route":
  - Day 0: Africa route completely shut down (port/political issue in West
  Africa)                                                                       
  - Day 0: Hormuz at 15% simultaneously                                         
  - Day 0: Red Sea at 20% simultaneously                                        
                                                                                
  Now there is no safe route. Gulf and Russia are both disrupted, Africa is
  gone. The UAE pipeline (if the feature flag were on) would be the only clean  
  option.         
                                                                                
  ---             
  7. houthi_red_sea
  Directly based on the 2024 real-world Houthi attacks. Red Sea drops to 5%     
  capacity from day 0 for the full duration. Ships carrying Russian oil via Suez
   are badly hit. Gulf shipments are completely unaffected because they use     
  Hormuz, not the Red Sea.
                                                                                
  ---             
  8. russia_sanctions
  Russia supply is available for the first 60 days, then gets sanctioned and cut
   off completely for the remaining 40 days. No chokepoint disruptions — the
  issue is purely political. Tests whether the remaining Gulf + Africa capacity 
  is enough to cover the gap.
                                                                                
  ---             
  9. tanker_strike
  A combined hit on day 30:
  - Hormuz drops to 30% (moderate disruption)
  - 25 tankers are permanently destroyed simultaneously                         
                                                       
  Tests whether the fleet can absorb a sudden capacity loss while a chokepoint  
  is also congested.                                                            
                                                                                
  ---                                                                           
  10. stochastic_conflict ← the one you asked about
                                                                                
  This one has no fixed events. Instead, disruptions happen randomly throughout
  the simulation. The randomness is controlled by three parameters:             
                  
  ┌────────────┬────────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   
  │ Parameter  │ Value  │                      Meaning                      │
  ├────────────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────┤   
  │ Mean       │ 60     │ On average, a new disruption happens every 60     │
  │ interval   │ days   │ days. Could be sooner, could be later — drawn     │   
  │            │        │ randomly.                                         │   
  ├────────────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │            │        │ Each disruption is randomly somewhere between     │   
  │ Severity   │ 0.1 –  │ mild (0.7 = 30% slower) and severe (0.1 = 10×     │   
  │ range      │ 0.7    │ slower). You don't know how bad it'll be until it │
  │            │        │  hits.                                            │   
  ├────────────┼────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Mean       │ 45     │ Each disruption lasts around 45 days on average,  │
  │ duration   │ days   │ again randomly.                                   │   
  └────────────┴────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                                                                                
  The chokepoint that gets hit is also random. So in one replication you might  
  get a mild 20-day Red Sea disruption on day 40. In another you might get a
  severe Hormuz blockade on day 10. That's why we run 40 replications — to      
  average out the randomness.

  This is the most realistic scenario because real geopolitical disruptions     
  don't follow a fixed schedule. It's also the scenario where adaptive strategy 
  shines most — because it responds to whatever actually happens rather than    
  being calibrated for one specific event.                   
                                                                                
● Here's every feature flag explained simply:                                   
                                                                                
  ---                                                                           
  1. Tanker Breakdowns                                                          
  enable_tanker_breakdowns | default: OFF                                       
                                                                                
  Ships break down randomly during their voyage. The rate is controlled by MTBF 
  (Mean Time Between Failures) — default 8,760 hours = once a year per tanker on
   average. When a breakdown happens, the tanker stops for a random repair time 
  (default ~14 days mean). During that time it's out of service. Turns the fleet
   from a perfect machine into something more realistic where you occasionally
  lose a ship temporarily.

  Sub-parameters:
  - tanker_mtbf_hours — how often breakdowns happen. Lower = breaks down more
  often                                                                         
  - tanker_repair_mean_hours — how long repairs take. Default 336h = 14 days
                                                                                
  ---                                                                           
  2. Demand Elasticity
  enable_demand_elasticity | default: OFF                                       
                                         
  In real life, when a country is running low on oil, it rations. Factories slow
   down, power plants switch fuels, people drive less. This flag models that.   
  When inventory gets low, demand automatically reduces:
                                                                                
  - Inventory above 30% of max → full 4.2M bbl/day demand (no change)           
  - Inventory 10–30% of max → demand drops to 85% (15% rationing)
  - Inventory below 10% of max → demand drops to 60% (emergency rationing)      
                  
  Without this flag, the simulation assumes India always consumes exactly 4.2M  
  bbl/day even when stocks are nearly empty — which is pessimistic but simpler.
                                                                                
  ---             
  3. Seasonal Weather
  enable_seasonal_weather | default: OFF                                        
                                        
  The Indian Ocean has a monsoon season from roughly May to November. During    
  this period, bad weather slows ship transit times. At peak (September), ships 
  take up to 30% longer to complete voyages. The effect follows a smooth sine
  curve — gradually increases from May, peaks in September, then fades by       
  November. Outside the monsoon window, no effect.

  ---
  4. Panic Ordering
  enable_panic_ordering | default: OFF                                          
                                      
  Models the bullwhip effect — the real-world tendency for buyers to over-order 
  when they see a disruption coming. When a chokepoint disruption is detected,  
  instead of dispatching the normal 3 tankers per order cycle, the system
  dispatches 3× as many (controlled by panic_multiplier, default = 3, so up to 9
   tankers at once).

  This sounds helpful but actually makes things worse — you flood the system    
  with orders, tankers all arrive together and overfill inventory, then nothing
  is ordered for a long time, then inventory crashes again. Classic bullwhip    
  oscillation.    

  Sub-parameter:
  - panic_multiplier — how many times the normal dispatch rate. Default 3
                                                                                
  ---
  5. Information Delay                                                          
  enable_information_delay | default: OFF
                                         
  In reality, India's oil inventory figures aren't updated in real time.
  Refiners, traders, and policymakers are working off data that is hours or days
   old. This flag simulates that. The ordering policy doesn't see the current
  inventory — it sees what inventory was X hours ago.                           
                  
  Sub-parameter:                                                                
  - information_delay_hours — how stale the data is. Default 24 hours
                                                                                
  Effect: the system reacts late. If inventory is falling fast, the order
  trigger fires later than it should, because the policy thinks inventory is    
  higher than it actually is. This can push the system into shortage before it
  even places an order.                                                         
                  
  ---
  6. SPR Auto-Refill
  enable_spr_refill | default: OFF
                                  
  By default the SPR only ever goes down (releases during emergencies). With    
  this flag on, it automatically refills during calm periods — if commercial    
  inventory is healthy and the SPR isn't full, it tops up at 500K bbl/day. Much 
  slower than the 0.8M bbl/day release rate, so it takes weeks to meaningfully  
  refill.         

  Without this flag, the SPR is a one-time buffer. With it, the SPR gradually   
  rebuilds between crises.
                                                                                
  ---             
  7. Freight Dynamics
  enable_freight_dynamics | default: OFF
                                        
  When the fleet is heavily utilised (lots of tankers busy), shipping companies 
  charge more — basic supply and demand for ship capacity. This flag adds a     
  price premium on top of the base freight cost when fleet utilisation goes     
  above 70%.                                                                    
                  
  At 70% utilisation → no extra cost. As utilisation climbs toward 100%, the    
  premium grows quadratically — at 100% utilisation the cost is up to 3× the
  base freight rate. Reflects real charter market conditions where a crisis     
  causes a shipping rate spike on top of the supply disruption itself.

  ---
  8. Pipeline Bypass (UAE)
  enable_pipeline_bypass | default: OFF
                                       
  The UAE has a real pipeline called the ADCOP (Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline)   
  that runs overland from Abu Dhabi to Fujairah on the east coast — bypassing   
  the Strait of Hormuz entirely. This flag activates it as a supply source.     
                                                                                
  The pipeline doesn't turn on immediately. The simulation waits until Hormuz   
  has been severely disrupted (below 30% status) for 10 days, then activates it
  — modelling the real-world delay in diverting supply infrastructure. Once on, 
  it provides oil at $4.00/bbl with no chokepoint risk, but smaller cargo sizes
  (500K bbl per delivery vs 1.5M bbl for a tanker).

  Without this flag, Gulf_Pipeline is always disabled and never used — which is 
  why you see 0 Gulf_Pipeline deliveries in all the base results.
